# CULT1001 / History of Asia — Source Notes

## Murphey, *A History of Asia* — Key Passages for Japan/East Asia Essays

### Chapter 9: Early Japan and Korea (pp. 183–206)
- Prince Shōtoku missions, Seventeen-Article Constitution (604), Taika Reforms (645): pp. 183–200
- Nara period (710–784), Heian period (794–1185): pp. 200–215
- Lady Murasaki, Tale of Genji, Pillow Book: pp. 1040–1090 of extracted text (PDF pp. ~194–200)
- Tea ceremony and Tang China preservation: "anyone who wants to get a glimpse of what Tang China was like must go to Japan" — approx. p. 195

### Chapter 12: Tokugawa Japan (pp. 255–267)
- Sakoku, isolationism, Dutch at Nagasaki, 1639 edict: pp. 255–265
- Late Tokugawa national consciousness, Western medical knowledge, Dutch anatomy comparison (1771): pp. 265–270
- **Key quote** (exact, Ch. 12, pp. ~267–270):
  > "They were able to adopt ideas and techniques from foreign sources, as they long had done from China, without in any way diluting their own cultural and national identity."
  - Attribution context: late Tokugawa period, Japanese national consciousness developing under Tokugawa unification. Frame this quote as Tokugawa-era identity formation, not Meiji per se.
  - Paraphrase must preserve **"in any way"** — it is an emphatic qualifier, not filler. Dropping it understates the claim.

### Course materials — what to cite and what to avoid

**Not acceptable as standalone scholarly sources:**
- Module lecture PDFs/slides (e.g. `Module_08_Early_Japan_and_Korea.pdf`)
- WSU branded reading packs
- Course discourse analysis documents

**Why:** These are teaching materials, not peer-reviewed scholarship. WSU's "5 scholarly sources" requirement expects proper academic sources.

**What to do instead:**
1. Replace module citations with the **primary source anthologies** cited within them (Kublin for Shōtoku Constitution, Ebrey for Tang Taizong memoranda — both are properly published academic collections)
2. Keep the **textbook** (Murphey) as primary evidence
3. If unsure whether a source qualifies, look for: author affiliation (university press), peer review statement, DOI or ISBN

**Format for replacing course materials:**
```
Kublin, Hyman, ed. Japan: Selected Readings. Boston: Houghton Mifflin, 1968.
  [Primary sources: Prince Shōtoku, Seventeen-Article Constitution (604 CE); 
   Tang Taizong, Memoranda on Governance (648 CE).]

Ebrey, Patricia Buckley, ed. Chinese Civilization: A Sourcebook. 2nd ed. 
  New York: The Free Press, 1993.
```

### Chicago Notes-Bibliography Format (17th ed.)

### Book with chapter spans
```
Murphey, Rhoads, and Kristin Stapleton. A History of Asia. Routledge, 2019.
  Chapter 9, "Early Japan and Korea," 183–206; Chapter 12, "Tokugawa Japan," 255–267.
```
**Common error:** Listing "Chapter 9" only when the essay uses content from BOTH chapters. Always list each chapter actually cited.

### Course module / lecture slides (teaching materials)
```
Western Sydney University. CULT1001: Asia in the World — Module 08: Early Japan and Korea. 2026.
```
These are acceptable undergraduate sources but are not peer-reviewed primary scholarship. Supplement with textbook evidence.

### Discourse analysis (course resource)
```
Western Sydney University. CULT1001: Asia in the World — Discourse Analysis: Prince Shōtoku's
  Seventeen-Article Constitution (604 CE) and Tang Taizong's Memoranda on Governance (648 CE). 2026.
```

## Essay QA Thresholds (CULT1001)
- Body word count: 1,200–1,250 (strict 1,200 target; 50w buffer acceptable)
- Paragraph length: 49–100 words (split any >100w)
- No single-sentence paragraphs
- No semi-colons, contractions, dot points
- No quotation over ~20 words
- Student ID + page numbers in document header
- **Full assignment question in the document header** — not an abbreviated topic line. Write it out in full so the reader can see exactly what was asked. Update the header metadata when the question is confirmed.
