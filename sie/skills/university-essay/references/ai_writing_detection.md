# AI-Generated Academic Writing: Detection Markers

Use this checklist when the user asks "does this sound like a student wrote it?" or "is this AI-generated?" — **after** standard QA vocabulary/formatting checks pass.

## Structural Tells

- **Uniform paragraph rhythm** — every paragraph: claim → citation → concept → wrap-up. Real student writing is messier; some paragraphs luxuriate, others rush.
- **Source name-dropping** — cites Bourdieu, Giddens, Tarrow in sequence without *doing* anything original with the theory. A real student picks one or two frameworks and actually uses them.
- **Conclusion mirrors introduction** near-verbatim. Students usually drift or add nuance.
- **Every insight is attributed** — zero original analysis. A good student essay uses theory to say something the theorist didn't say.
- **No counterargument that isn't immediately absorbed** — the essay never risks a claim it can't defend.

## Voice Tells

- **No personal register** — even one sentence of positionality ("as someone who..." or "having seen...") is a strong student signal. Its absence in a whole essay is an AI signal.
- **Academic vocabulary without colloquial slip** — real students occasionally use a slightly awkward but authentic phrasing. AI output is consistently polished.
- **"This framing does real work"** — reads like an essay-writing guide, not a student's voice.
- **Emphatic clichés** — "Protest is not a threat to order. It is how democratic societies change." is a TED talk closer, not a student conclusion.
- **Safe conclusion** — restates the thesis with a flourish but risks nothing. A real student might end with a question, a tension, or a concrete implication.

## Sentence-Level Tells

- **Metrically consistent sentence length** — AI sentences cluster around 20–28 words. Students vary more.
- **No sentence fragments for effect.**
- **Parallel structures that are too tidy** — "These laws target dissent. These laws silence marginalised groups. These laws protect economic interests." → textbook AI.

## The Student Voice Test

Ask: **"Would a marker at an Australian university believe a 20-year-old wrote this?"**

If the answer is no, the fixes are:

1. **Add "I" positions** — "I was initially sympathetic to X until..." Markers expect critical self-positioning.
2. **Let one paragraph be messy** — pick the weakest section and complicate it. What if the framework doesn't fully explain the case?
3. **Cut one textbook citation, replace with own observation** — the most skippable "theory name-drop" paragraph.
4. **Vary sentence length aggressively** — start one paragraph with a 5-word sentence. End another with a 40-word qualifier.
5. **Make the conclusion risk something** — a question, a tension, an admission of limitation. Never restate the thesis.
6. **Add one "I don't know" or "this is harder to resolve than it seems"** — intellectuals admit uncertainty. AI never does.

## When This Checklist Applies

- User asks "is this human?" / "does this sound like AI?" / "would a marker flag this?"
- After writing an essay, as an additional QA pass (after AI-humanizer, before final delivery)
- When reviewing someone else's draft for authenticity

## When NOT to Apply

- Do NOT use this checklist to reject a draft outright. Use it to generate actionable humanisation edits.
- Do NOT apply to the user's own prose when they've said "use my wording."
- Do NOT apply when the user specifically wants polished AI-generated output.
